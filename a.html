Abstract

This analysis will apply Linear Mixed Effects Modelling to critically compare a Random Intercept Model( users nested within 12 countries) with a Random Intercept+ Slope Model allowing varying effects of ‘Milestones’ across countries. Then, stochastic analysis will determine the smallest sample needed for future trials having 90% power. It will conclude with strategic recommendations based on both analyses.

Analysis

Random intercepts model (RIM)

Methodological justification & specification

It is an appropriate model because the outcome variable is continuous, unlike a t-test, that isn’t sensitive to geographic differences, by applying “complete pooling”, the RIM acknowledges users nested in 12 different countries by ‘partial pooling’. For example, users in Spain may engage more than users in Belgium regardless of feature, RIM applies the global average to stabilize estimates for countries with greater variance, which reduces over-fitting of the estimates(Ramírez, 2021).

The model formatted as an equation:

‘app_useij​ = γ00​ +β1​ms_feat.fij​ + u0j​ + ϵij’​

  1. app_useij : usage for user ‘i’ in country ‘j’

  2. γ00: average usage-control group

  3. β1: Fixed effect-milestone feature (fixed slope)

  4. u0j: Random Intercept for country ‘j’: displacement from global average

  5. ​ϵij: Individual residual-error

RIM fit allows countries to vary in their baseline engagement to generalize for population variability. This enables the product analyst to evaluate the ‘true’ milestone feature-effect.

 app_rim <- lmer(app_use ~ ms_feat.f +  (1|country), data = app)

Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC)

A statistical tool measuring the proportion of total variance explained by cluster effects(country-level differences in engagement) (Wu , 2024).

Random Intercepts + Slopes Model (RISM)

Methodological Justification & Specification

In contrast to RIM, that assumes the ‘Milestones’ feature causes a homogenous increase in engagement, the RISM allows Milestone’s influence to vary(by adding random slopes). This enables product analysts to capture realistic insights due to unaccounted local market trends that could consecutively impact engagement, reducing chances of a type-1 error(Heisig, 2019). 

In the equation for RISM, (u1j ) is added to the RIM equation, which accounts for  the country-specific Milestones effect(slope). The new model can be equated as:

app_useij​= γ00​ ​+ (β1​+u1j​)(ms_featij​)+ u0j​+ ϵij

app_rsm <- lmer(app_use ~ 1 + ms_feat.f + (1+ms_feat.f|country), data = app)

Part II: Stochastic Power Analysis

Method Justification

Testing the minimum sample requirement for 90% power based on RISM for future trials. The stochastic methods can handle mixed and multiple random effects that generally causes a standard power analysis to collapse when dealing with clustered data (Brysbaert, 2018) . It assumes the future variance structures to resemble the current dataset’s.

# Create data index
index <- 1:nrow(app)
# Empty dataframe
sim_results1 <- data.frame(
  n_ppts = NULL,p_val = NULL)
# Counter
row_counter1 <- 0
#  sample sizes
for (n in seq(100, 1200, 90)) {
  for (i in 1:1000) {
    # Resample rows
    resample_index1 <- sample(index, size = n,
                             replace = TRUE)
    # Create resampled dataset
    resample_dat1 <- app[resample_index1, ]
    # Fit model
    new_mod1 <- lmer(app_use ~ ms_feat.f + (1 + ms_feat.f | country),
      data = resample_dat1)
    # Store results
    row_counter1 <- row_counter1 + 1
    sim_results1[row_counter1, "n_ppts"] <- n
    sim_results1[row_counter1, "p_val"] <-
      summary(new_mod1)$coefficients[
        "ms_feat.f1",
        "Pr(>|t|)" ]}}
# Calculate power for each set of participants
sim_power1 <- sim_results1 %>% 
  group_by(n_ppts) %>% 
  summarise(power = mean(p_val < .05))

Critical Evaluation and Conclusion

The main positive strength of the fitted mixed-effects models is accounting for nested structure(countries) of the data, which often gets overlooked in t-tests. Although both RIM and RISM found a significant relationship of milestone effect on app engagement, the RISM meaningfully captured data better and is comparatively practical for planning effective strategies.

The findings of RIM could be oversimplified, due to its restrictive modelling assumptions. When a random slope was added, residual errors reduced from 220 to 166.6 . Antonakis, (2019) explains that RIM assumes a homogenous feature-effect, hence the differences between countries gets masked and consecutively increases individual residual-error. This assumption could be unrealistic given figure.1 & figure.5 showing only UK and Poland having declined engagement for test group. 

Additionally, when RISM was fitted, standard error increased from 0.3 to 4.9. Theoretically it reflects lower statistical precision and greater uncertainty,  but it provides a more realistic estimate because the model doesn’t assume homogenous feature-effects globally(Bristol, 2026). Furthermore, the ICC of RISM(58%) being higher than the RIM(43%), indicated that more variability was recognised as country-level rather than purely individual noise, showing how feature-effect  is regionally subjective.  

Imagining the perspective of a product analyst, the RISM presented more detailed insights. Since it depicted how countries like Spain and Italy had greater positive response on engagement compared to others (steeper slopes as seen on figure.5), Instagrammer may strategically benefit from fast-tracking roll-out and investment on marketing in these regions. Whereas countries like Portugal and UK, urges analysts to investigate them further for macro-market conditions or local competitors(having similar features) for incentivising Instagrammar to stand-out. Although complex, this shows how RISM is the better model, as supported by the LRT.

Power analysis revealed that larger samples of 1000 is required to account for substantial between-country variances of feature-effect and to stabilise parameter uncertainty. However it is important to recognize a few limitations. Theoretically, 30-50 clusters(countries) are recommended to gain reliable stability of heterogenous feature-effects(slopes)(Wiley, 2018).  Although not considered problematic by the quality check, a small proportion of singular-fit and convergence warnings were reflected in the analysis; future alternative recommendations could be Bayesian analysis as it handles data with few clusters well(McNeish, 2016).  

Furthermore, engagement was recorded only for the first week. This means the findings cannot reflect whether engagement is sustained or if some regions are slower/faster to accept new features in the market. To conclude a mixed effects model capturing longitudinal data over a few months and includes more clusters to evaluate feature-effect on engagement can enable the product analyst to plan even reliable and effective localised strategies.
